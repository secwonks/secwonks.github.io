The threat intelligence industry has a output problem. Organizations spend considerable resources on feeds, platforms, and analyst hours—and produce reports that sit unread in inboxes while the same decisions get made without them.

This is not primarily a data quality problem. It is a requirements problem, an integration problem, and in many cases, a structural incentives problem.

## The Requirements Failure

Effective intelligence starts with requirements: specific, answerable questions from decision-makers who will act on the answers. Most programs start instead with collection—building data pipelines and only then asking what questions the data might answer.

The result is intelligence that is accurate, timely, and irrelevant.

## Closing the Loop

The framework that works is deceptively simple: work backwards from decisions. Identify the decisions your security organization needs to make in the next 30, 90, and 180 days. For each decision, specify what information would change the outcome. Collect against those requirements. Assess and produce against those requirements. Measure whether decisions actually changed.

This sounds obvious. Almost no programs do it.

## On Indicators of Compromise

IOCs are not intelligence. They are data. The conflation of IOC feeds with threat intelligence programs is perhaps the industry's most expensive category error—expensive in dollars, in analyst time, and in the false confidence that comes from indicator matching in a SIEM.

Genuine intelligence about adversary behavior—TTPs, targeting priorities, operational patterns—degrades far more slowly and informs architectural decisions rather than alert queues.
